MASALA BOLLYWOOD ROMANCE CORPUS
Source: Original screenplay excerpts in the style of 1990s-2000s Bollywood romance
Genre: Romance | Era: Masala/Commercial Peak | Region: Bollywood

EXT. EUROPEAN COUNTRYSIDE - TRAIN - DAY

A train through Switzerland. Green hills, blue sky,
everything impossibly pretty.

In the corridor: RAJ, 24, loud, charming, slightly
drunk on the view, spots SIMRAN, 22, trying to
read in the last compartment.

He drops into the seat across from her.
She looks up. She does not look thrilled.

                    RAJ
          Is anyone sitting here?

                    SIMRAN
          No.

                    RAJ
          Good. Because I've been standing
          for three hours and—

                    SIMRAN
          I said nobody is sitting here.
          Meaning you are welcome to leave me alone.

                    RAJ
          Right. Of course. Absolutely.

He does not leave. He looks out the window.

                    RAJ (CONT'D)
          Beautiful country.

                    SIMRAN
          Yes.

                    RAJ
          You Indian?

                    SIMRAN
          Yes.

                    RAJ
          Thought so. London?

                    SIMRAN
                    (despite herself)
          How did you know?

                    RAJ
          The way you said yes. Like you're
          not sure the question deserved it.

She goes back to her book. Raj grins.
The train goes through a tunnel.
For a long moment they are in darkness together.

EXT. SWISS ALPS - MUSTARD FIELDS - DAY

The fields are in bloom. Yellow forever.

Simran runs through them. She can't help it.
The fields demand running. She has her arms out.

From a hill above, Raj watches.
He's seen a lot of things. He hasn't seen this.

He doesn't call out to her. He just watches.

Later, she'll say the moment wasn't romantic.
He'll say he knew exactly what it was
the second he saw it.

They're both right.

INT. YOUTH HOSTEL - SIMLA - NIGHT

A dining table full of backpackers. Raj at one
end, telling a story that has everyone laughing.
Simran at the other end, pretending not to listen.

                    KAVITA
                    (to Simran)
          Your friend is funny.

                    SIMRAN
          He's not my friend.

                    KAVITA
          Your... enemy?

                    SIMRAN
          He's nobody. He's just on the same train.

Kavita looks between them.

                    KAVITA
          If you say so.

Raj is still talking. His eyes cross the table
and find Simran for a half-second.

She looks away. So does he.

EXT. HEIDELBERG - OLD TOWN - DAY

Walking through cobblestone streets. They have
been walking for two hours and haven't run out
of things to argue about.

                    SIMRAN
          I'm telling you, it's better to plan.
          Spontaneous people miss things.

                    RAJ
          Spontaneous people FIND things.
          Look — this street. I didn't plan this street.

                    SIMRAN
          I did. I read about this street.
          In a guidebook. Which I brought.
          In a bag I packed carefully.

                    RAJ
          You planned this beautiful moment
          we're having?

                    SIMRAN
          We're not having a moment. We're having
          a disagreement about travel planning.

                    RAJ
          Sometimes those are the same thing.

She stops at a bakery window. Looks at the cakes.

                    SIMRAN
          My father has found someone for me.
          Back home. That's why I came on this trip.
          One last—

She stops.

                    RAJ
          One last?

                    SIMRAN
          Adventure. I don't know why I told you that.

                    RAJ
          Because I'm nobody. Remember?
          Nobodies are safe to tell things to.

She looks at the cakes. Then at him. Then back.

                    SIMRAN
          His name is Kuljeet. He works in my
          father's friend's business.

                    RAJ
          And?

                    SIMRAN
          And he's perfectly nice.

The silence says everything the words don't.

INT. TRAIN - NIGHT

They are at the ends of a compartment. Everyone
else is asleep. The train rocks.

                    RAJ
          I was going to go back.

                    SIMRAN
          To India?

                    RAJ
          My father wants me to come home.
          Take over the shop. He's not well.

                    SIMRAN
          Then you should go.

                    RAJ
          I know.

                    SIMRAN
          Why haven't you?

He looks out the window. Dark fields going by.

                    RAJ
          I kept thinking there was one more thing
          to see. One more country. One more train.

                    SIMRAN
          And now?

He looks at her.

                    RAJ
          Now I think I found the thing.
          Which is inconvenient.

                    SIMRAN
          Very.

EXT. LONDON - HEATHROW AIRPORT - DAY

The terminal. The departure board clicking.
Her parents. His bag.

SIMRAN'S FATHER, 55, formal, shakes Raj's hand.

                    MR. CHAUDHARY
          Thank you for looking after her.

                    RAJ
          She mainly looked after herself, sir.

Mr. Chaudhary nods. He's assessing.

                    MR. CHAUDHARY
          You're Raj from Punjab?

                    RAJ
          My father has a shop in Southall, sir.

                    MR. CHAUDHARY
          I know the shop. Good family.

He turns away. Raj looks at Simran over her
father's shoulder.
She gives him nothing. She gives him everything.
They are not the same look.

She goes through security.
He stands at the barrier.
He should leave. He knows he should leave.

He stays.

EXT. PUNJAB - MUSTARD FIELDS - DAY

A different mustard field. Same yellow.
Raj walks through it and finds this absurd
considering.

A TRAIN passes a kilometer away on an elevated track.
He looks at it.

Then he starts walking toward the village.
He knows where she is. He's been standing outside
that knowledge for three weeks.

Time to walk in.

INT. CHAUDHARY HOUSE - LIVING ROOM - EVENING

Simran at the window. The engagement preparations
happening around her. Fabric samples. Guest lists.
Her MOTHER excited. Her cousins everywhere.

She sees him out the window.

He's standing at the gate. Hat in hand.
He looks exactly the same as in Europe except
he's also completely different, because he came here.

She doesn't move.

He doesn't move.

Her mother notices.

                    MOTHER
          Simran? Who is that?

A pause. A very long pause.

                    SIMRAN
          Someone I met on a train.

She goes to the front door. Opens it.
The evening light is behind him like he planned it.
He definitely didn't plan it.

                    RAJ
                    (quietly)
          Your father said good family.

                    SIMRAN
                    (equally quiet)
          He said good family.

                    RAJ
          I was thinking perhaps—

                    SIMRAN
          Come inside. Meet my father.

She holds the door open.
He walks through.
It's the bravest thing either of them has ever done.

EXT. SWISS ALPS - DAY

RAHUL runs through the field of yellow flowers. PRIYA runs away from him. This is their language — pursuit and retreat that both enjoy.

                    RAHUL
          Stop running!

                    PRIYA
          You stop chasing!

Neither stops.

INT. PRIYA'S HOSTEL ROOM - NIGHT

Priya writes in her diary. Stops. Looks at the ceiling.

                    PRIYA (V.O.)
          He is annoying. He talks too much.
          He borrowed my umbrella and returned
          it broken. I do not like him.

She smiles at the ceiling.

EXT. ZURICH TRAIN STATION - DAY

The train to London. Priya's father waits on the platform. He has come to take her home. An arranged marriage is waiting.

Priya looks at the platform. Looks at the train. Looks at the flowers she is still holding from this morning.

Rahul is not here.

The whistle blows.
