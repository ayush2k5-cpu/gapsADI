INT. HYDERABAD CENTRAL POLICE STATION - NIGHT

The station hums with late-night activity. INSPECTOR KRISHNA (40s, lean, weathered) sits at his desk surrounded by case files. A single bulb swings overhead. He lights a cigarette and stares at a photograph — a body found near Charminar.

                    KRISHNA
          Three bodies. Same method.
          No witnesses. No motive.
          At least, not one anyone
          will say out loud.

CONSTABLE VENKAT (20s, fresh from training, eager) places a tea glass on the desk.

                    VENKAT
          Sir, the lab report came.
          The toxicology — it is not
          what we expected.

Krishna does not look up.

                    KRISHNA
          It never is, Venkat.
          It never is.

EXT. CHARMINAR MARKET - EARLY MORNING

The old market awakens. Vendors lay out cloth and produce. RAZIA BEGUM (60s, sharp-tongued, a fixture of the market for forty years) arranges her stall. She watches a black Ambassador car idle at the lane mouth.

                    RAZIA
                (to herself)
          That car has been there
          every morning this week.
          These men do not buy cloth.

She memorises the number plate.

INT. COMMISSIONER'S OFFICE - DAY

COMMISSIONER NARAYANAN (55, silver-haired, politician's smile) reviews the case file across from Krishna. Between them on the mahogany desk — the toxicology report.

                    NARAYANAN
          I want this closed, Krishna.
          Quietly. The press is already
          sniffing. Election season
          is not a good time for
          unsolved murders.

                    KRISHNA
          Three murders, sir. With
          the same poison compound.
          This is not a coincidence —

                    NARAYANAN
          It is a coincidence until
          I say otherwise. Close it.

Krishna places the file back on the desk.

                    KRISHNA
          Yes, sir.

He does not mean it.

EXT. MUSI RIVERBANK - LATE AFTERNOON

Krishna meets an informant — SALIM (30s, nervous, a small-time fixer) — by the concrete steps leading to the river. Salim speaks without making eye contact.

                    SALIM
          The three men who died — they
          were not random. They all attended
          a meeting six months ago.
          A meeting about land in Cyberabad.
          Land that is now worth ten times
          what it was.

                    KRISHNA
          Who called that meeting?

                    SALIM
          If I tell you that, I end up
          number four on your list.

He stands and walks away.

INT. KRISHNA'S APARTMENT - NIGHT

A small flat. Maps pinned to one wall. Krishna plots the locations of all three deaths — they form a triangle around a single property parcel in the city's outskirts.

He circles the parcel. He writes a name beneath it.

He stares at the name for a long time. Then he picks up the phone.

EXT. CYBERABAD CONSTRUCTION SITE - NIGHT

Bulldozers silent in the darkness. Krishna moves through the site with a torch. He finds a metal box buried near the foundation markers. Inside — documents. Land survey records with altered boundary lines.

A sound behind him. He spins.

RAJAN (40s, a former colleague, now in plainclothes) stands at the edge of the torchlight.

                    RAJAN
          You should not have come here, Krishna.

                    KRISHNA
          You should not have been part of this.

A long silence between old friends.

INT. POLICE STATION HOLDING ROOM - DAWN

Rajan sits across from Krishna. No recording equipment. No other officers.

                    RAJAN
          I did not order the killings.
          You have to believe that.
          I just arranged the meetings.
          I did not know what Narayanan
          intended.

                    KRISHNA
          Three men are dead, Rajan.
          Your ignorance does not
          bring them back.

                    RAJAN
          What will you do?

Krishna opens the metal box on the table between them.

                    KRISHNA
          The only thing left to do.

EXT. HYDERABAD HIGH COURT - MORNING

Krishna walks up the stone steps of the court building. The documents are in a sealed envelope under his arm. Behind him, a black Ambassador car follows at a distance.

He does not look back.

INT. HIGH COURT CORRIDOR - CONTINUOUS

A clerk leads Krishna to a judge's antechamber. He hands over the envelope.

                    KRISHNA
          These go on record today.
          Whatever happens to me
          after I leave this building —
          this does not disappear.

The clerk nods. Stamps the envelope.

EXT. HIGH COURT STEPS - CONTINUOUS

As Krishna descends the steps, two men in suits approach him from either side. He stops.

The black Ambassador idles at the kerb.

                    KRISHNA (CONT'D)
                (quietly)
          Took you long enough.

He raises his hands. He is not afraid.

INT. COMMISSIONER'S OFFICE - SAME TIME

Narayanan receives a phone call. His face changes.

                    NARAYANAN
                (into phone)
          What do you mean it is already
          filed? How did he — when did —

He stands. Knocks the tea glass from his desk.

                    NARAYANAN (CONT'D)
          Get me the Chief Minister's office.
          Now.

EXT. HYDERABAD STREETS - DAY

The city continues its morning. Traffic. Chai stalls. School children. A newspaper vendor stacks copies of the evening edition — the headline is not yet written.

But it will be.
