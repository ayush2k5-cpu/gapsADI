CLASSIC HOLLYWOOD THRILLER CORPUS
Source: Original screenplay excerpts in the style of 1940s-50s film noir
Genre: Thriller/Film-Noir | Era: Classic/Golden Age | Region: Hollywood

INT. INSURANCE OFFICE - DAY

WALTER NEFF, 35, lean and sharp-eyed, leans back in his
chair dictating into a wire recorder. His voice is flat.
Tired. Like a man confessing to a priest he doesn't believe in.

                    WALTER (V.O.)
          I killed Dietrichson. Me, Walter Neff,
          insurance salesman. Thirty-five years old,
          unmarried, no visible scars — until a while ago,
          that is.

He clicks off the recorder. Stares at the ceiling.

INT. DIETRICHSON HOUSE - LIVING ROOM - DAY

Spanish colonial. Dark wood, heavy drapes. PHYLLIS
DIETRICHSON, 28, descends the stairs wrapped in a towel,
surprised to find someone waiting.

                    PHYLLIS
          I was just sunning myself on the terrace.
          You're from the insurance company?

                    WALTER
          Pacific All-Risk. Your husband called about
          renewing his automobile policy.

She moves past him, picking up a cigarette from the table.
She lights it slowly. Everything she does is slow. Deliberate.

                    PHYLLIS
          He's not here. He's at the office.

                    WALTER
          I see.

He watches her. She knows he's watching.

EXT. DIETRICHSON HOUSE - DRIVEWAY - DAY

Walter walks to his car. He looks back at the house once.
Something has happened to him in the last fifteen minutes.
He doesn't know what yet.

INT. KEYS' OFFICE - NIGHT

BARTON KEYES, 50s, stocky, rumpled, sits across a desk
covered in actuarial tables and claim files. He talks the
way he breathes — constantly.

                    KEYES
          You know what the double indemnity clause says?
          Two times the face value in case of accidental
          death. Two times. You know what that adds up
          to, Walter?

                    WALTER
          A hundred thousand dollars.

                    KEYES
          A hundred thousand dollars. And the little man
          in here—

He taps his chest.

                    KEYES (CONT'D)
          The little man is talking to me. He always
          talks to me when something's wrong.

EXT. JERRY'S MARKET - NIGHT

Rain. A parking lot slick as a mirror. Walter waits by
a lamppost, collar up. Phyllis appears from the shadows,
moving fast.

                    WALTER
          You're late.

                    PHYLLIS
          I couldn't get away sooner. He was watching me.

                    WALTER
          He's always watching you. That's his job.

                    PHYLLIS
          Don't say it like that.

                    WALTER
          How should I say it?

She stops. Faces him. The rain doesn't bother her.

                    PHYLLIS
          Walter. Are you losing your nerve?

                    WALTER
          I never had any nerve. That's the difference
          between us.

INT. TRAIN - OBSERVATION CAR - NIGHT

Walter sits in the dark. The train rocks. He checks his
watch. He's thinking about the body in the back.
About Keyes. About Phyllis.

About all the ways this goes wrong.

EXT. TRAIN TRACKS - NIGHT

The train passes through darkness. Signal lights blink.
A maintenance crew works on the far track with lanterns.

INT. TRAIN - OBSERVATION CAR - CONTINUOUS

Walter rises. He moves through the empty car. He knows
what he has to do.

                    WALTER (V.O.)
          I kept thinking about Keyes and his little man.
          About how the little man was probably talking
          to him right now. I thought about Phyllis.
          About how I'd been played. I thought about
          the last forty-eight hours. And then I stopped
          thinking.

EXT. TRAIN - NIGHT

Walter drops to the tracks. The train moves away.

INT. KEYS' OFFICE - DAY

Keyes stands at the window. Behind him, Walter sits.

                    KEYES
          Know what I keep thinking about? The trolley
          car. In every double indemnity murder, there's
          always a loose end. Just one. Like a trolley
          car running on a fixed track. You can see
          where it's going from the beginning. You just
          can't get off.

He turns. Looks at Walter a long moment.

                    KEYES (CONT'D)
          You look terrible, Walter.

                    WALTER
          Yeah. I know.

INT. INSURANCE OFFICE CORRIDOR - NIGHT

Empty. Only the hum of ventilation. Walter walks slowly
toward the elevator. Behind him —

Keys steps out of his office.

                    KEYES
          Walter.

Walter stops. Doesn't turn.

                    KEYES (CONT'D)
          I know it was you. I've known for three days.

A very long silence.

                    WALTER
          Why didn't you call?

                    KEYES
                    (quietly)
          Because I kept hoping I was wrong.

Walter turns around. He looks at Keyes. Something in
him gives out. He leans against the wall.

                    WALTER
          Closer than that, weren't we, Keyes?

                    KEYES
          I never figured you for it, Walter.
          Never in a million years.

EXT. BUILDING ENTRANCE - NIGHT

Walter pushes out through the glass doors into the night.
He doesn't run. There's nowhere to run to.

INT. DINER - NIGHT

A counter. Two stools. NINO ZACHETTI, 25, sits with
black coffee going cold in front of him. He's been
waiting a long time for someone who isn't coming.

The WAITRESS refills his cup without asking.

                    NINO
          You seen a woman in here tonight? Blonde.
          Wearing a red scarf.

                    WAITRESS
          Honey, in this neighborhood, that could
          be half the women in town.

She moves away. Nino wraps his hands around the cup.
He knows something he doesn't want to know.

INT. PHYLLIS' HOUSE - STUDY - NIGHT

Phyllis sits in darkness. The only light comes from
the street. She's very still. Like she's been still
for a long time.

Walter enters. He doesn't turn on the light.

                    WALTER
          It's over, Phyllis.

                    PHYLLIS
          I know.

                    WALTER
          I'm going to Keyes. Tomorrow morning.

                    PHYLLIS
          Are you going to tell him about me?

                    WALTER
          I don't know yet.

She rises. Moves toward him. In the darkness, she
puts her hand against his chest.

                    PHYLLIS
          I never loved you, Walter.

                    WALTER
          I know that too.

A beat.

                    WALTER (CONT'D)
          Then why does it feel like you did?

EXT. CITY STREET - DAWN

Walter walks. The city is waking up around him.
Delivery trucks. A newspaper boy. A woman walking
a dog. Everything ordinary.

He walks past a phone booth. Stops. Goes back.
Picks up the receiver.

                    WALTER (V.O.)
          I called Keyes at six in the morning.
          He picked up on the first ring. 
          He'd been awake, of course.
          The little man had been keeping him up all night.

INT. KEYS' OFFICE - MORNING

Keyes sits at his desk. Walter sits across from him.
Between them: a single yellow legal pad.
Walter begins to talk.

He talks for a long time.
