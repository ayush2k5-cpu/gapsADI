PARALLEL CINEMA INDIAN DRAMA CORPUS
Source: Original screenplay excerpts in the style of 1970s-80s Indian parallel cinema
Genre: Drama | Era: New Wave/Parallel Cinema | Region: Indian

INT. POLICE STATION - NIGHT

Fluorescent light. Paint peeling. ANANT WELANKAR, 35,
Inspector, sits at his desk in full uniform.
A FAN turns slowly overhead. He's been here twelve hours.

A CONSTABLE brings tea. Anant wraps his hands
around the glass. He's reading a case file
he's read thirty times.

                    CONSTABLE
          Sir, the Union men are calling again.

                    ANANT
          Tell them I'm in a meeting.

                    CONSTABLE
          With whom, sir?

He looks at the empty desk across from him.

                    ANANT
          With myself. It's the only meeting
          that matters.

EXT. DHARAVI CHAWL - MORNING

A settlement of cramped rooms and open drains.
Children playing between lines of washing.
A WOMAN cooks on a kerosene stove in the corridor.

Anant walks through, out of uniform. Nobody greets him.
He is not from here. He grew up not far away,
but that's a different world.

                    OLD MAN
                    (from a doorway)
          Inspector sahib. Looking for someone?

                    ANANT
          Ramya. Ramya Bai's room.

                    OLD MAN
          Third floor. Last door.
                    (pause)
          She's not been well.

INT. RAMYA'S ROOM - DAY

A small room. A cot, a trunk, a calendar.
RAMYA, 50s, sits on the cot wrapped in a shawl.
She looks at Anant without surprise.

                    RAMYA
          They sent a policeman now?

                    ANANT
          I came on my own. Nobody sent me.

                    RAMYA
          Sit down then. Don't stand there
          like you want something from me.

He sits on the edge of the trunk.

                    ANANT
          Your son Soma. He's been charged.
          Mill lockout protest. Lathi charge.
          He hit a constable.

                    RAMYA
          He defended himself.

                    ANANT
          That's not how it reads in the FIR.

                    RAMYA
          The FIR. Yes.
                    (she looks at him)
          Tell me, Inspector. Have you ever read
          an FIR and believed every word of it?

A pause.

                    ANANT
          No.

                    RAMYA
          Then you know what the FIR is.

INT. MILL GATE - DAY

Workers lined up outside the locked gate.
Signs and slogans. SOMA, 28, stands at the front.
He's not shouting. He's just standing there.

Anant watches from a distance. He's not in uniform.

When the police arrive in trucks, Anant steps back
into the crowd. He watches the lathis come out.
He watches Soma stand his ground until he can't.

He doesn't move to intervene.

He doesn't know why.
He knows why.

INT. COMMISSIONER'S OFFICE - DAY

Freshly painted. Nice curtains. COMMISSIONER DANGE
sits behind a clean desk.

                    DANGE
          Welankar. You've been seen at the mill.
          In civilian clothes.

                    ANANT
          I was gathering information, sir.

                    DANGE
          Who authorized this?

                    ANANT
          I authorized myself, sir. The Union case—

                    DANGE
          The Union case is handled. The men will
          be processed and released. In two weeks.
          Elections are coming, Welankar.
          Use your head.

Anant looks at the curtains. They are the same
curtains as last year. Only cleaner.

                    ANANT
          Soma Ramya is twenty-eight years old, sir.
          His father died in that mill.

                    DANGE
          Many fathers died in many mills,
          Inspector. That's what mills are for.

EXT. CHAUPATI BEACH - EVENING

Anant walks along the water. The city behind him:
noise and light and ten million small dramas.

He sits on the sand. He has his badge in his hand.
He looks at it for a long time.

A CHILD comes and sits nearby, making something
in the sand. The child doesn't look at him.

Anant puts the badge back in his pocket.

INT. LOCK-UP - NIGHT

A cell. Soma sits against the wall. When Anant
enters alone, Soma doesn't rise.

                    SOMA
          You're here to tell me I'll be released.
          In two weeks.

                    ANANT
          Yes.

                    SOMA
          And my colleagues?

                    ANANT
          The same.

                    SOMA
          And the mill?

                    ANANT
          The lockout stands.

Soma nods slowly.

                    SOMA
          My father worked that mill for twenty years.
          I was born while he was on a shift.
          He died on a shift.
                    (pause)
          I thought that meant something.
          About belonging to a place.
          About what you owe it.

                    ANANT
          It means something.

                    SOMA
          Then why are you on that side of the bars?

The question sits between them.
Anant has no answer. Not one he can say out loud.

EXT. POLICE STATION - MIDNIGHT

Anant stands outside smoking. His shift is over.
He's still here.

A CONSTABLE comes out.

                    CONSTABLE
          Sir, going home?

                    ANANT
          In a minute.

The constable goes back in. Anant finishes his smoke.

He's thinking about Soma's question.
He's thinking about his uniform, hanging inside.
He's thinking about what his father did for a living,
before the city swallowed everything.

He drops the cigarette. Goes back in.

He has four more hours of paperwork. And then
he'll go home. And nothing will have changed.
Except something in him, which is worse.

INT. POLICE STATION - NIGHT

SUB-INSPECTOR ANANT VELANKAR, 35, fills out a report. His handwriting is neat. He takes care with forms. Around him, controlled chaos — drunks, complainants, constables.

A woman enters. KAMLA, 40s, sari torn at the shoulder. She stands at the gate, unsure.

                    ANANT
          Come in.

She approaches the desk. Does not sit.

                    KAMLA
          My husband. He—

                    ANANT
          Sit down.

She sits.

                    KAMLA
          He beats me. Since ten years.

Anant writes. Does not look up.

                    ANANT
          Any witnesses?

                    KAMLA
          The neighbours. But they won't
          say anything.

                    ANANT
          Any medical reports?

                    KAMLA
          He won't let me go to the doctor.

Anant stops writing. Looks at her for the first time.

EXT. CHAWL - NIGHT

Anant stands outside Kamla's building. He can hear shouting. Second floor.

He has no warrant. He has no authority here — it's another division's territory.

He goes up anyway.

INT. KAMLA'S HOME - NIGHT

Her husband VISHNU, 45s, opens the door. Sees the uniform. His expression shifts — fear into performance.

                    VISHNU
          Inspector. What brings you—

                    ANANT
          Step outside.

                    VISHNU
          This is my house. I have rights—

                    ANANT
          Step outside.

Vishnu steps outside.

INT. POLICE STATION - SENIOR OFFICER'S ROOM - DAY

Inspector GAIKWAD, 50s, reads Anant's report without expression.

                    GAIKWAD
          You entered the residence without
          a warrant.

                    ANANT
          The woman was at risk.

                    GAIKWAD
          That's your assessment.

                    ANANT
          Yes.

                    GAIKWAD
          Her husband has influence.
          The corporator is his cousin.

Anant says nothing.

                    GAIKWAD
          Withdraw the complaint. Let it
          be a domestic matter.

                    ANANT
          It is a domestic matter. That
          doesn't make it not a matter.
