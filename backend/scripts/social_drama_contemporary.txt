SOCIAL DRAMA CONTEMPORARY INDIAN CORPUS
Source: Original screenplay excerpts in the style of 2010s-present Indian social realism
Genre: Drama | Era: Contemporary | Region: Indian

INT. DISTRICT COURT - WAITING AREA - MORNING

Plastic chairs. A tube light that's been flickering
since the previous government. People with cases
that have been pending long enough that they
know which chair squeaks.

RATI DEVI, 35, sits with a file in her lap.
She wears a sari that was pressed this morning.
She has a small notebook. She has been writing
in the notebook.

Her LAWYER, SIDDHARTH RAJAN, 40, sits beside her,
drinking chai from a paper cup in the distracted
way of someone who drinks a lot of chai.

                    SIDDHARTH
          Your husband's side will argue provocation.

                    RATI
          There was no provocation.

                    SIDDHARTH
          I know that.

                    RATI
          He hit me because he was angry.
          He was angry because the cricket match
          went badly.

                    SIDDHARTH
          I know.

                    RATI
          His team lost. And therefore—

                    SIDDHARTH
          I know, Rati-ji. But the court needs—

                    RATI
          I have a bruise here.
                    (she indicates her arm under the sleeve)
          And here.
                    (her collarbone)
          The doctor—

                    SIDDHARTH
          The medical report is filed. But proceedings
          take time. The first hearing today is just—

                    RATI
          How long?

A pause. He drinks chai.

                    SIDDHARTH
          Two years. If it goes smoothly.

                    RATI
          Two years.

                    SIDDHARTH
          Possibly less, with the right judge—

                    RATI
          And in these two years—

                    SIDDHARTH
          You can go to your father's house.

                    RATI
          My father's house has my father and my
          father's opinion of women who leave
          their husbands. Even these husbands.

She opens her notebook. She writes something.
The tube light flickers.

                    RATI (CONT'D)
          I'll wait.

INT. DISTRICT COURT - COURTROOM - DAY

The JUDGE, 55, adjusts her glasses. The room
is full of pending-case energy — everyone
has somewhere else to be in theory.

RATI'S HUSBAND, ARUN, 38, sits across with his
lawyer. He looks at her once. His expression
is not guilt. It's something Rati recognizes.
It's inconvenience.

                    JUDGE
          Case number CR-2847. Patialwala vs.
          Patialwala. Section 498A.

                    ARUN'S LAWYER
          Your Honor, the alleged incidents—

                    SIDDHARTH
          Your Honor, we have submitted
          medical evidence—

                    JUDGE
          One at a time.
                    (pause)
          This is a first hearing. We will establish
          basic facts today. Nothing more.

She looks at both parties. She has seen this
case ten thousand times. She has never stopped
seeing each version of it separately.

EXT. DISTRICT COURT - OUTSIDE - AFTERNOON

Rati sits on a wall eating a roti she brought
from home. Siddharth has a samosa.

                    SIDDHARTH
          Today went fine.

                    RATI
          Today nothing happened.

                    SIDDHARTH
          In court, nothing happening is fine.

                    RATI
          My daughter is at my sister's house.
          She doesn't know where I am.

                    SIDDHARTH
          You haven't told her?

                    RATI
          She's nine. What do I tell a nine-year-old?

                    SIDDHARTH
          That you're solving a problem.

Rati eats her roti. Looks at the court building.

                    RATI
          She looks like him. My daughter.
          Same eyes. Same way of turning
          her head when she thinks.

                    SIDDHARTH
          That's—

                    RATI
          One of the things nobody tells you.
          The children carry all of it. Everything
          you loved about him. Everything that
          made this possible.
                    (pause)
          She still asks when Papa is coming home.

The samosa. The roti. The court building.

                    SIDDHARTH
          What do you tell her?

                    RATI
          That we're figuring it out.

INT. RATI'S SISTER'S HOUSE - EVENING

A house full. Three children. A husband at work.
PRIYA, Rati's sister, 30, is cooking. Rati
sits at the kitchen table watching.

                    PRIYA
          Mother called.

                    RATI
          I know.

                    PRIYA
          She wants you to consider—

                    RATI
          No.

                    PRIYA
          Rati—

                    RATI
          Priya. I know what she wants me to consider.
          I've considered it. My answer is no.

Priya cooks. The sound of the tadka in oil.

                    PRIYA
          He sent a card. For Rekha's birthday.

                    RATI
          Rekha's birthday is in three weeks.

                    PRIYA
          I know. He sent it early.
          It has two hundred rupees in it.

Rati looks at her hands on the table.

                    RATI
          She'll want to thank him.

                    PRIYA
          Yes.

                    RATI
          I'll tell her she can.

She gets up. Takes over the tadka without being asked.
Priya steps aside.

                    RATI (CONT'D)
          I'm not doing this to punish him, you know.
          I'm not doing it out of anger.

                    PRIYA
          I know.

                    RATI
          I'm doing it because Rekha is watching
          everything. Every single thing.
          She is watching what I do and she is
          learning what is normal.
                    (pause)
          I won't let this be normal.

She cooks. Her hands are steady.

REKHA, 9, comes into the kitchen and looks up
at her mother.

                    REKHA
          Mama. Who is winning?

                    RATI
          We are.

                    REKHA
          Who is 'we'?

Rati looks at her daughter. Those familiar eyes.

                    RATI
          You and me.

She plates the food. Sets the table.
One place for Priya's family. One for Rekha.
One for herself.

Three plates. Nobody missing.

INT. DISTRICT LOWER COURT - DAY

RANI, 28, domestic worker, sits in the waiting area. She has been here since 7am. It is now 2pm. Her case has been called three times and postponed three times.

Beside her: ADVOCATE FATIMA, 35, junior lawyer, two files and not enough sleep.

                    FATIMA
          The next date will be in three
          months. November.

                    RANI
          My daughter's school—

                    FATIMA
          I know.

                    RANI
          They suspended her. Because I
          filed the case. The employer's
          son-in-law is on the school board.

Fatima writes something in her file. Does not look up.

                    FATIMA
          Noted.

                    RANI
          You said that last time.

INT. EMPLOYER'S BUNGALOW - DAY (FLASHBACK)

RANI mops the floor. MRS. SHARMA, 55s, watches from the sofa.

                    MRS. SHARMA
          You're slow today.

                    RANI
          I'm sorry, madam. My daughter
          was unwell last night.

                    MRS. SHARMA
          That's your problem. Not mine.
          I'm paying for work, not excuses.

Rani keeps mopping.

                    MRS. SHARMA
          And the silver spoon from the
          dining set. It's missing.

Rani stops.

                    RANI
          I haven't taken anything, madam.

                    MRS. SHARMA
          I'm not saying you did. I'm saying
          it's missing.

Same thing. And they both know it.

EXT. DISTRICT COURT STEPS - EVENING

Rani sits on the steps. The court is closed. Fatima sits beside her.

                    FATIMA
          We have a good case.

                    RANI
          You keep saying that.

                    FATIMA
          Because it's true.

                    RANI
          The truth is I need the job more
          than I need the case. My daughter
          needs the school. My landlord
          needs the rent.

Fatima is quiet.

                    RANI
          But I also need—

She stops. Looks at the court building.

                    RANI
          I just need someone to say
          it happened. That it matters
          that it happened.

INT. COURTROOM - DAY

Three months later. Rani stands. The judge reads from the file.

The courtroom is almost empty. No press. No spectators.

Just the judge, the lawyers, Rani, and the truth, waiting to be decided.

INT. LEGAL AID OFFICE - DAY

ADVOCATE MENON, 55, reads Rani's file. Does not look enthusiastic.

                    MENON
          Harish Mathur is their counsel.
          Seventeen adjournments in the last case.

                    RANI
          I don't have seventeen adjournments.
          I have school fees and a landlord.

                    MENON
          We have the law.

                    RANI
          The law has been on my side since November.

Menon has no answer for this.

EXT. COURT STEPS - EVENING

Fatima catches up to Rani on the steps.

                    FATIMA
          Next date is March.

Rani looks at the court building one more time.

                    RANI
          Every time I come here I think
          it will be different.

She walks. Does not wait for an answer.
