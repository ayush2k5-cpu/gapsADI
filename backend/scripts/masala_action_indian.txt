MASALA ACTION BOLLYWOOD CORPUS
Source: Original screenplay excerpts in the style of 1980s-2000s Indian commercial action cinema
Genre: Action | Era: Commercial Peak/Masala | Region: Indian

EXT. VILLAGE - NIGHT

Everything is burning.

RAMKISHAN, 60, a farmer, runs from his own house
with a child in his arms. GABBAR SINGH's men —
on horseback, rifles firing — circle the village.

THAKUR, 55, the village headman, stands in the square.

                    THAKUR
                    (roaring)
          GABBAR! Show yourself like a man!

A shot. Thakur falls. Not dead. But fallen.

His son VEERU, 8 years old, watches from a ditch.

                    VEERU'S MOTHER
                    (calling)
          Veeru! Come! Come now!

But Veeru is watching his father on the ground.
He will not forget this image for the rest of his life.

EXT. MOUNTAIN ROAD - DAY (TWENTY YEARS LATER)

A bus winds up a narrow mountain road.

On top of the bus: JAI, 28, handsome and easy,
sits with his legs hanging over the edge,
coin spinning in his hand.

Inside the bus: VEERU, 28, big and loud,
makes conversation with everyone who doesn't
want to talk to him.

The bus stops. A bandit team blocks the road.

Look at Jai. He's already standing.
Look at Veeru. He's already grinning.

                    VEERU
          Ten of them.

                    JAI
          I count eleven.

                    VEERU
          I'll take six.

                    JAI
          You take five. You always lose count.

                    VEERU
          I do not lose count.

They look at each other. The BANDITS wait,
confused by this conversation.

                    JAI
          After you.

                    VEERU
          No, after you.

                    JAI
          Veeru.

                    VEERU
          What?

                    JAI
          On three.

                    VEERU
          On three? Why three? Why not—

                    JAI
          THREE.

They move. It's over in about a minute.
The bandits don't know what hit them.
Neither do the passengers.

EXT. RAMGARH VILLAGE - DAY

Thakur — older now, quieter — watches the two
men arrive. He's been waiting.

                    THAKUR
          I've heard of you. Both of you.
          The newspapers call you criminals.

                    JAI
          The newspapers call everyone something.

                    THAKUR
          I want to hire you.

                    VEERU
          We're not for hire, Thakur Saheb.

                    THAKUR
          For this you will be.
                    (pause)
          Gabbar Singh is still in these mountains.
          He has been here for twenty years.
          Burning. Killing. Taxing.
          Nobody stops him.

                    JAI
          Why don't the police stop him?

                    THAKUR
          The police are afraid.

                    VEERU
          Then we're definitely interested.

Jai shoots him a look.

                    JAI
          We haven't agreed to anything.

                    VEERU
          I agreed.

                    JAI
          You agreed for both of us?

                    VEERU
          We're partners. That's how partners work.

                    JAI
                    (to Thakur)
          He spends my money the same way.
          How much?

INT. GABBAR'S MOUNTAIN CAMP - NIGHT

Fire. Rocks. Armed men everywhere.

GABBAR SINGH, 45, massive and wrong-eyed, sits
on a throne of stone. Someone has made a mistake.
A BANDIT kneels before him.

                    GABBAR
          Kitne aadmi the?

                    BANDIT
          Sardar... do teen...

                    GABBAR
                    (soft)
          Do teen. Sirf do teen aadmi.
          Aur hamare pachas aadmi...?

He looks at his hands.

                    GABBAR
                    (still soft)
          Jo darr gaya, samjho mar gaya.

He picks up a revolver. Spins the cylinder.

The fire cracks. The other bandits watch.
Nobody says anything.

EXT. RAMGARH - WELL - AFTERNOON

BASANTI, 22, talks. She's been talking for
fifteen minutes without a noticeable pause.
VEERU sits at the well. He was trying to draw water.

                    BASANTI
          My mother always said if you talk to
          a man at the well before noon it means
          you'll marry him. Which is silly because
          I've talked to the water-seller every
          morning since I was six and I have
          not married him. Although he did ask once.
          I said no obviously. His horse is much
          worse than Dhanno. My Dhanno is the
          best horse in this district. Ask anyone.

                    VEERU
          I believe you.

                    BASANTI
          You're the strangers Thakur hired.

                    VEERU
          We're not strangers for long.

She looks at him. He's smiling that impossible smile.

                    BASANTI
          Don't do that.

                    VEERU
          What?

                    BASANTI
          Smile like that. It's not useful.

He keeps smiling. She picks up her water pot.
She walks away fast. She's smiling too.
She won't let him see that.

EXT. VALLEY PASS - DUSK

Jai sits on a rock playing his harmonica.
Veeru sits beside him, tired.

                    VEERU
          You think we can do this?

                    JAI
          We've done harder things.

                    VEERU
          Gabbar has forty men.

                    JAI
          We've fought forty men before.

                    VEERU
          Not all at once.

                    JAI
          No. That was new.

A pause. The harmonica again.

                    VEERU
          Jai. If something happens—

                    JAI
          Nothing's going to happen.

                    VEERU
          But if it does—

                    JAI
                    (stopping)
          Then nothing happens to you, understand?
          You have people here now. Basanti.
          Don't be dramatic.

                    VEERU
          I'm not being dramatic. I'm being—

                    JAI
          Dramatic.

                    VEERU
          Practical.

Jai starts playing again. The sun goes down
over the mountains. Somewhere up there, Gabbar's
fires are starting to burn.

EXT. RAMGARH VILLAGE - CLIMAX - DAY

Gunfire everywhere. Dust and chaos.
Veeru fights through the village, bleeding,
still moving.

Jai is down. He's been down for three minutes.
He's trying to get back up.

In the dust, through the noise, Veeru sees him.

                    VEERU
          JAI! JAI GET UP!

He can't hear. Or he hears and can't.

Veeru fights his way through. When he reaches
Jai, he drops down beside him. Puts his hand
on his chest. Jai looks up at him.

                    JAI
          The coin.

He holds out a coin. His coin. The one he
always spins.

                    JAI (CONT'D)
          It was always heads.
                    (barely)
          Both sides.

Veeru takes the coin.

He can't speak. Which is the only time
in recorded history that Veeru cannot speak.

Jai closes his eyes.

EXT. RAMGARH VILLAGE - AFTER - DAY

The sun. The smoke clearing. The village intact.

Thakur approaches Veeru. Both of them are
looking in the same direction.

                    THAKUR
          He was a good man.

Veeru is holding the coin. He doesn't say anything.
Then:

                    VEERU
          He was the best man.

He puts the coin in his pocket.

                    VEERU (CONT'D)
          Let's go finish this.

EXT. VILLAGE - DAWN

Smoke. The policemen have come in the night. Three houses burned. VIKRAM watches from the tree line, face unreadable.

                    VIKRAM
          They came for me.

His uncle RAGHU, 60s, touches his shoulder.

                    RAGHU
          Then go. Before they come again.

                    VIKRAM
          I will go. And I will come back.
          Not as nephew. As consequence.

He walks into the forest without looking back.

INT. CITY UNDERWORLD DEN - NIGHT

BHAI, 40s, slick suit, holds court. Twenty men. One chair. He sits, they stand.

                    BHAI
          Someone killed four of my men
          last night. On the highway near
          Sonpur. Professional work.

Silence.

                    BHAI
          Find this man and bring him to me.
          I want to know who taught him.
