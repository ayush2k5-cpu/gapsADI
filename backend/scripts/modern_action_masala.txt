MODERN MASALA ACTION CORPUS
Source: Original screenplay excerpts in the style of 2020s South Indian pan-India mass action cinema
Genre: Action | Era: Contemporary | Region: Indian (Pan-India)

EXT. BRITISH COLONIAL INDIA - FOREST - 1920 - DAWN

A forest that doesn't want to be entered.
Ancient trees. Mist that hasn't moved since yesterday.

Two soldiers enter. BRITISH OFFICERS: rifles out,
walking the way men walk when they've forgotten
they can die.

A sound. Stop.

Then BHEEM emerges from the trees — not walking,
materializing — like the forest itself decided
to become a man.

BHEEM (BHIMRAO ALLURI), 28, Gond tribesman,
moves with the silence of someone who grew up here.
He carries nothing that isn't him.

                    BRITISH OFFICER
          Halt! Who are you?

Bheem looks at them. He considers the question.

                    BHEEM
          Someone you should let pass.

They don't let him pass.

It doesn't go well for them.

EXT. DELHI - BRITISH RESIDENCY - DAY

A different world. Red brick. Flags. Ceremony.

RAM CHARAN RAJPUT, 28, inspector of the British
Indian constabulary, stands in a line of officers
being reviewed. He's the brightest one.
He's also the one who belongs least.

He knows this. He carries it like water in cupped hands —
carefully, accepting that some will spill.

                    BRITISH COMMISSIONER
          Rajput — exemplary record. You'll go far.

                    RAM
          Thank you, sir.

                    BRITISH COMMISSIONER
          Provided, of course, that you continue
          to understand which side of history
          you're standing on.

                    RAM
          I understand perfectly, sir.

He doesn't move. His face doesn't move.
Inside: something burning down low, carefully managed.

EXT. RIVER - DAY

A crowd at the river's edge. Chaos. A child
has fallen in.

The river is fierce. Crocodile water.

RAM is on the bridge above. He has a rope
and calculation and about eight seconds.

BHEEM is on the near bank. He has his hands
and the river and zero hesitation.

They both move at the same time.

In the water: they both have the child.
They look at each other.

For a moment, just two men. The child between them.
No uniforms. No tribal markings. No history.

Then the current, and they swim.

EXT. RIVER BANK - CONTINUOUS

They lay the child on the bank. The crowd
gathers around. The child is breathing.

                    BHEEM
                    (in his dialect)
          You're quick for a city man.

                    RAM
                    (in Hindi)
          You're reckless for someone
          with no rope.

                    BHEEM
          The river and I have an understanding.

                    RAM
          The river doesn't negotiate.

                    BHEEM
          It does if you ask correctly.

They look at each other. Agreement without words.
The beginning of something.

INT. DELHI BAZAAR - DAY

Ram and Bheem. They don't know each other's names.
They've decided not to say them.

Walking through the market.

                    BHEEM
          What do you do?

                    RAM
          Administration.

                    BHEEM
          You regulate things.

                    RAM
          I keep order.

                    BHEEM
          And this order — it's good? For everyone?

Ram looks at the market. This market where
his people sell things to survive and the
British skim from every transaction.

                    RAM
          It's order.

                    BHEEM
          That's not what I asked.

                    RAM
          I know.

INT. BRITISH RESIDENCE - BALLROOM - NIGHT

A function. White uniforms. Crystal.
Ram in full dress, standing at the margins.

Bheem is also here. Disguised. Looking for someone.
Looking for the child he came to Delhi to free.

Across the room they see each other.

At the same moment they both understand:
they have been on opposing missions every day
they've known each other.

                    BHEEM
                    (across the room, quietly)
          Who sent you here tonight?

                    RAM
                    (equally quiet)
          My duty.

                    BHEEM
          That's not an answer.

                    RAM
          I know.

EXT. FOREST - DAWN - LATER

Two men sitting across a fire. It's late enough
that pretense would be exhausting.

                    RAM
          I knew who you were. For two weeks.

                    BHEEM
          And you said nothing.

                    RAM
          And you? You knew I was a constable.

                    BHEEM
          I knew you were something to do with
          the British.

                    RAM
          But you didn't leave.

                    BHEEM
          You saved a child.

A pause. The fire between them.

                    RAM
          I have to bring you in, Bheem.

                    BHEEM
          I know.

                    RAM
          There will be soldiers here by morning.

                    BHEEM
          I know.

                    RAM
          Then why are you still sitting here?

Bheem looks at him across the fire.

                    BHEEM
          Because you're still sitting here.

A very long silence.

                    RAM
          If I let you go tonight—

                    BHEEM
          I'll finish what I came for.

                    RAM
          And then?

                    BHEEM
          And then whatever happens, happens.

Ram looks at the fire. He has a career.
He has a plan — one that will take years,
that involves sacrifice on every step.
He has been building toward something.

He thinks about the child in the river.
He thinks about the market. About what keeping
order actually keeps.

He stands up.

                    RAM
          The eastern pass. It's unwatched
          until 6am.

He doesn't look back. He walks into the trees.

Bheem watches him go. Then he rises.

He goes west. Not east.
He wants Ram to have something true to report
to his British masters. The east.

He owes him that much.

EXT. FOREST - SUNRISE

Ram stands at the eastern pass. Waiting.
At 6am, the soldiers arrive.

                    SOLDIER
          He came this way, sir?

                    RAM
          This way.

They search. They will find nothing.

Ram looks at the forest. He knows
Bheem went west. He knew when he said east.

He tells himself he did his duty.
He tells himself this for a long time.

He doesn't entirely believe it.
That's how he knows he's still alive inside.

EXT. FOREST - NIGHT

BHEEM moves through the trees like river water — no sound, no wasted movement. He carries a child on his back, unconscious, limp.

Behind him: torches. Fifty men. Getting closer.

He does not run faster. He changes direction.

INT. COLONIAL OFFICER'S BUNGALOW - NIGHT

SCOTT, 40s, British, sits with his brandy. His aide enters.

                    AIDE
          He escaped the forest cordon, sir.
          With the child.

Scott sets down the brandy.

                    SCOTT
          How many men?

                    AIDE
          Fifty.

                    SCOTT
          Against one tribesman.

Long pause.

                    SCOTT
          Double the reward. And find me
          someone who knows those forests
          better than he does.

EXT. DELHI STREET - DAY

RAJU on his motorcycle, weaving through traffic. He sees something across the street that makes him brake hard.

A man he recognises. A man he has been looking for.

He dismounts. Straightens his collar.

                    RAJU
          Finally.
